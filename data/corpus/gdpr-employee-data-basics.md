# GDPR and Employee Personal Data

The General Data Protection Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2016/679, the GDPR) governs the processing of personal data within the European Union, including personal data of employees and job applicants. Employers act as **controllers** when they decide why and how employee data is processed, and they carry the associated legal obligations.

## Lawful basis for processing

Every processing activity needs a lawful basis under Article 6. In the employment context, **consent is rarely an appropriate basis** because of the imbalance of power between employer and employee — the European Data Protection Board has stressed that consent is unlikely to be "freely given." Employers more commonly rely on:

- **Performance of the employment contract** (e.g. paying salary, administering benefits).
- **Compliance with a legal obligation** (e.g. tax, social security, health-and-safety reporting).
- **Legitimate interests**, balanced against the rights and freedoms of the employee.

Processing of **special-category data** (Article 9) — such as health, trade-union membership, or biometric data — requires an additional condition, often a specific provision in employment, social-security, or social-protection law.

## Core principles

Article 5 requires that personal data be processed lawfully, fairly and transparently; collected for specified, explicit and legitimate purposes (**purpose limitation**); limited to what is necessary (**data minimisation**); accurate; kept no longer than necessary (**storage limitation**); and processed securely (**integrity and confidentiality**). Controllers must be able to demonstrate compliance (**accountability**).

## Transparency and information

Employees must be informed, usually through a privacy notice, about what data is collected, the purposes and lawful bases, retention periods, recipients, and how to exercise their rights. This applies from recruitment onward.

## Employee rights

Employees have rights of **access**, **rectification**, **erasure**, **restriction**, **data portability**, and the right to **object** to certain processing. These rights are not absolute and may be limited where other legal obligations apply.

## Monitoring at work

Workplace monitoring (email, internet, CCTV, location tracking) must be necessary and proportionate. Employers should conduct a **Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA)** where monitoring is likely to result in a high risk to employees, and apply least-intrusive methods.

## Security, breaches and transfers

Controllers must implement appropriate technical and organisational measures. Personal data breaches must generally be reported to the supervisory authority **within 72 hours**, and affected individuals notified where there is a high risk. International transfers of employee data outside the EEA require an appropriate safeguard such as an adequacy decision or standard contractual clauses.

## National variation

Article 88 allows Member States to set more specific rules for employment data, so national law and works-council agreements often add requirements. This summary is factual and general and is not legal advice.
