# French Labor Law Overview

French employment law is codified primarily in the Labour Code (*Code du travail*), complemented by industry-wide collective agreements (*conventions collectives*) and company-level agreements. It is generally protective of employees and distinguishes carefully between contract types and modes of termination.

## The 35-hour week

The statutory **legal working time is 35 hours per week**. This is not a maximum but a threshold: hours worked beyond 35 are **overtime** (*heures supplémentaires*), paid at an increased rate (typically +25% for the first eight hours and +50% thereafter, subject to agreements). Absolute ceilings apply — generally **10 hours per day** and **48 hours per week** (44 hours on average over 12 weeks). Many employees, especially managers (*cadres*), work under a **forfait jours** arrangement counting days rather than hours.

## Employment contracts

The default and preferred contract is the open-ended contract (*contrat à durée indéterminée*, **CDI**). Fixed-term contracts (*contrat à durée déterminée*, **CDD**) are permitted only for specific, legally defined reasons (e.g. replacing an absent employee, a temporary increase in activity) and are limited in duration and renewals; an unjustified CDD may be reclassified as a CDI. End-of-CDD payments include a precarity bonus (*prime de précarité*).

## Paid leave

Employees accrue **2.5 working days of paid leave per month worked**, amounting to **30 working days (five weeks) per year**. Additional rest days (*RTT*) may arise where weekly working time exceeds 35 hours under an annualisation agreement. France also provides numerous statutory leaves for family events.

## Termination and dismissal

Dismissal (*licenciement*) requires a **real and serious cause** (*cause réelle et sérieuse*), either personal (conduct or capability) or economic. A formal procedure must be followed, including a preliminary meeting (*entretien préalable*) and written notification. Dismissed employees with sufficient seniority are entitled to **statutory severance pay** (*indemnité de licenciement*) and a notice period. Unfair dismissals are subject to a capped indemnity scale (the "Macron scale").

## Probation and representation

Probationary periods (*période d'essai*) are capped by law according to job category. Employee representation operates through the **Social and Economic Committee** (*Comité social et économique*, CSE), mandatory in companies with at least 11 employees, with broader powers above 50 employees.

## Minimum wage

France sets a national hourly minimum wage, the **SMIC** (*salaire minimum interprofessionnel de croissance*), revised at least annually and automatically when inflation passes a threshold.
