# Dutch Labor Law Overview

Employment in the Netherlands is regulated mainly by Book 7, Title 10 of the Civil Code (*Burgerlijk Wetboek*), the Working Hours Act (*Arbeidstijdenwet*), and the Work and Care Act (*Wet arbeid en zorg*), supplemented by collective labour agreements (*CAO*). Dutch law combines flexibility with strong dismissal protection.

## Employment contracts

Contracts may be **fixed-term** (*bepaalde tijd*) or **open-ended** (*onbepaalde tijd*). Under the "chain rule" (*ketenregeling*), successive fixed-term contracts convert into an open-ended contract once they exceed **three contracts or three years** (with breaks shorter than six months counting toward the chain). On-call and zero-hours arrangements are subject to specific protective rules.

## Working time

The Working Hours Act sets limits such as a maximum of **12 hours per shift** and **60 hours per week**, with lower averages over longer reference periods (for example an average of 48 hours per week over 16 weeks). Employees are entitled to defined daily and weekly rest periods and to breaks during longer shifts.

## Paid leave

Employees accrue statutory paid holiday of **at least four times the weekly working hours per year** — for a full-time worker this equals **20 days (four weeks)**. Many CAOs add extra days. A **holiday allowance** (*vakantiegeld*) of at least 8% of gross annual salary is normally paid, typically in May.

## Notice and dismissal

Statutory **notice periods for employers** depend on length of service: **one month** for under five years, **two months** for 5–10 years, **three months** for 10–15 years, and **four months** for 15 years or more. Employees normally give one month's notice. Dismissal generally requires a valid ground and prior authorisation: through the **UWV** (the employee insurance agency) for economic or long-term-illness dismissals, or through the **sub-district court** (*kantonrechter*) for personal grounds.

## Transition payment

On termination at the employer's initiative (and in many other cases), employees are entitled to a statutory **transition payment** (*transitievergoeding*) from the first day of employment, calculated as **one-third of a month's salary per year of service**, subject to a statutory maximum.

## Sickness and parental leave

Employers must generally **continue paying at least 70% of wages for up to two years** during illness. The Netherlands also provides maternity, paternity, and parental leave under the Work and Care Act, partly paid through the social-security system.
