# German Labor Law Overview

German employment law (*Arbeitsrecht*) is built from several statutes rather than a single labour code, supplemented by collective agreements (*Tarifverträge*) and works-council co-determination. The core sources include the Civil Code (*Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch*, BGB), the Working Hours Act (*Arbeitszeitgesetz*, ArbZG), the Federal Leave Act (*Bundesurlaubsgesetz*, BUrlG), and the Dismissal Protection Act (*Kündigungsschutzgesetz*, KSchG).

## Working time

Under the Working Hours Act, the standard maximum is **eight hours per working day**, which may be extended to ten hours provided the average over six months (or 24 weeks) does not exceed eight hours per day. There must be at least **11 hours of uninterrupted rest** between shifts, and rest breaks of 30 minutes for work over six hours (45 minutes for work over nine hours). Sunday and public-holiday work is generally prohibited, subject to exceptions.

## Vacation

The Federal Leave Act guarantees a statutory minimum of **24 working days of paid annual leave** based on a six-day working week, equivalent to **20 days on a five-day week**. Many collective and individual contracts grant more, commonly 25–30 days. Employees with a recognised severe disability receive additional leave.

## Notice and termination

Statutory notice periods under the BGB start at **four weeks** (to the 15th or end of a calendar month) and increase with length of service — for example one month after two years, rising in steps up to seven months after 20 years of employment. The Dismissal Protection Act applies to businesses with more than ten employees and to staff with more than six months' tenure; in those cases a dismissal must be socially justified on grounds relating to the person, conduct, or operational needs (*betriebsbedingt*).

## Works councils and co-determination

Employees in establishments with at least five employees may elect a **works council** (*Betriebsrat*) under the Works Constitution Act (*Betriebsverfassungsgesetz*). Works councils have information, consultation, and co-determination rights over matters such as working-time arrangements, overtime, and certain personnel measures.

## Continued pay and sick leave

Employees are entitled to **continued payment of wages for up to six weeks** during illness (*Entgeltfortzahlung*). Thereafter, statutory health insurance pays sickness benefit. Statutory protections also cover maternity (*Mutterschutz*) and parental leave (*Elternzeit*).

## Minimum wage

Germany has a statutory **national minimum wage** (*Mindestlohn*), adjusted periodically by an independent commission, which sets a floor below which hourly pay may not fall for most workers.

This summary is factual and general and is not legal advice; specific entitlements depend on the applicable collective agreement and individual contract.
