# EU Working Time Directive (2003/88/EC)

The Working Time Directive (Directive 2003/88/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council of 4 November 2003) sets minimum health and safety requirements for the organisation of working time across the European Union. It applies to most sectors of activity, both public and private, with limited exceptions and sector-specific rules (for example for mobile transport workers).

## Maximum weekly working time

The Directive limits average working time to **48 hours per seven-day period**, including overtime. This limit is calculated as an average over a reference period that normally may not exceed four months. Collective agreements may extend the reference period, and Member States may permit individual workers to "opt out" of the 48-hour limit where national law allows, provided the worker consents freely and is not penalised for refusing.

## Daily and weekly rest

Workers are entitled to a **minimum daily rest period of 11 consecutive hours** in every 24-hour period. In addition, for each seven-day period a worker is entitled to a **minimum uninterrupted rest period of 24 hours**, which is added to the 11 hours of daily rest, producing a combined weekly rest of 35 hours. Where objective or technical conditions justify it, the weekly rest may be averaged over a 14-day period.

## Rest breaks

Where the working day is longer than six hours, every worker is entitled to a **rest break**. The details — duration and terms — are set by collective agreements or national legislation.

## Paid annual leave

Every worker is entitled to **paid annual leave of at least four weeks** (20 days for a five-day working week). This entitlement cannot, except where the employment relationship is terminated, be replaced by a payment in lieu. The Court of Justice of the European Union has consistently held that the right to paid annual leave is a particularly important principle of EU social law.

## Night work

Normal hours of work for **night workers** must not exceed an average of eight hours in any 24-hour period. Night workers whose work involves special hazards or heavy physical or mental strain must not work more than eight hours in any period during which they perform night work. Night workers are entitled to free health assessments.

## Derogations and scope

The Directive permits derogations for specific activities — such as security and surveillance, continuity of service (hospitals, utilities), and foreseeable surges in activity (agriculture, tourism) — provided that equivalent compensatory rest is granted. Member States transpose the Directive into national law and may set more protective standards.

The Directive is a floor, not a ceiling: national rules and collective agreements frequently provide more generous entitlements than the minimums described here. This summary is factual and general and is not legal advice.
