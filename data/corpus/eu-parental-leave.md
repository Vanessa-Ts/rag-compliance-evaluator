# EU Work-Life Balance Directive and Parental Leave

The Work-Life Balance Directive (Directive (EU) 2019/1158 of 20 June 2019) sets minimum standards across the European Union for parental, paternity, and carers' leave, and for flexible working arrangements for parents and carers. It repealed and replaced the earlier Parental Leave Directive (2010/18/EU) and aims to improve the reconciliation of work and family life and to encourage a more equal sharing of caring responsibilities between women and men.

## Paternity leave

Fathers — or, where recognised by national law, equivalent second parents — are entitled to **at least 10 working days of paternity leave** around the time of the birth of a child. This leave must be compensated at a level **at least equal to national sick pay**, and entitlement may not be made conditional on a length-of-service requirement.

## Parental leave

Each worker has an individual right to **at least four months of parental leave** to be taken before the child reaches a specified age (set by Member States, up to eight years). To promote equal sharing, **two of the four months are non-transferable** between parents — if a parent does not use them, they are lost. The Directive requires that an adequate payment or allowance be provided for at least the non-transferable portion, with the amount defined by Member States.

## Carers' leave

Workers are entitled to **at least five working days of carers' leave per year** to provide personal care or support to a relative, or to a person living in the same household, who needs significant care for a serious medical reason. Member States may set additional details and may, but need not, make this leave paid.

## Flexible working and time off for force majeure

Parents of children up to at least eight years old, and carers, have the right to **request flexible working arrangements** — including remote work, adjusted schedules, and reduced hours. Employers must consider and respond to such requests. Workers also have a right to **time off from work on grounds of force majeure** for urgent family reasons in cases of illness or accident, where their immediate presence is indispensable.

## Protection and non-regression

Workers who take or request these leaves are protected against **less favourable treatment and dismissal** connected to the leave. The Directive contains a non-regression clause: transposing it may not be used to reduce existing higher levels of protection in Member States. Member States transpose these minimums into national law and frequently provide more generous entitlements.
